Book Summaries

The Coaching Habit by Michael Bungay Stanier

Say less, ask more & change the way you lead forever
Influence And Motivation
Author

Michael Bungay Stanier

Michael Bungay Stanier’s “The Coaching Habit” isn’t just about coaching in a professional setting; it’s a guide to fostering better communication and unlocking potential in any relationship, including personal ones. The core premise revolves around the power of asking the right questions rather than providing answers. The book advocates for a shift from the “expert” mindset to a “coach” mindset, emphasizing empowering others to find their own solutions. This involves a deliberate and structured approach, built upon seven key questions and a supporting framework.

The book’s central advice is encapsulated in its title: cultivating a coaching habit. It’s not a one-off skill but a consistent practice requiring conscious effort and dedicated time. Stanier emphasizes that effective coaching isn’t about dispensing wisdom but about creating space for others to discover their own. He argues that this approach leads to greater ownership, commitment, and ultimately, better outcomes.

The seven core questions form the backbone of the coaching habit, designed to progressively guide the coachee toward self-discovery and action. These questions are not interchangeable; they follow a specific sequence designed for maximum impact:

  1. The Kick-off Question: “What’s on your mind?” This seemingly simple question is crucial for establishing rapport and allowing the coachee to set the agenda. It avoids assumptions and encourages open communication. The key is to listen attentively without interrupting or offering unsolicited advice. This initial question sets the stage for genuine connection and understanding.

  2. The AWE Question: “And what else?” This seemingly simple follow-up is incredibly powerful. It encourages deeper exploration, often revealing underlying issues and concerns that might not be immediately apparent. It’s about digging beneath the surface and uncovering the full complexity of the situation. Repeated use of this question allows for a richer, more complete understanding.

  3. The Focus Question: “What’s the real challenge here for you?” This question helps to pinpoint the core issue, cutting through distractions and focusing on the true problem that needs addressing. It forces the coachee to clarify their priorities and identify the central obstacle preventing progress. It moves the conversation beyond symptoms to underlying causes.

  4. The Foundation Question: “What do you want?” This question shifts the conversation from problems to desired outcomes. It helps to clarify goals and expectations, aligning the coachee’s efforts towards a specific, positive vision. It clarifies intentions and motives, shaping a focused direction for their efforts.

  5. The Lazy Question: “How can I help?” This seemingly passive question is deceptively powerful. It shifts the responsibility back to the coachee, prompting them to identify specific ways they can improve or receive assistance. It emphasizes self-reliance and prompts creative solutions. It’s a key strategy to avoid taking ownership of the coachee’s problems.

  6. The Strategic Question: “If you’re saying yes to this, what are you saying no to?” This question forces consideration of trade-offs and prioritization. It highlights the opportunity cost of choices, prompting a deeper reflection on resource allocation and commitment. It ensures that decisions are made strategically and purposefully.

  7. The Generative Question: “What’s the one thing you’re going to do?” This final question translates intentions into concrete action. It demands a commitment to a specific, measurable step, ensuring that the coaching session leads to tangible progress. It emphasizes accountability and encourages forward momentum.

Beyond the seven questions, Stanier provides several actionable insights:

  • The importance of listening: Active listening is paramount. It’s not just about hearing words but understanding the underlying emotions and perspectives. This involves paying close attention to both verbal and non-verbal cues.

  • The power of silence: Allowing for pauses and silence creates space for reflection and deeper thinking. It avoids the urge to fill empty space with advice or solutions.

  • The art of summarizing: Summarizing the coachee’s thoughts and feelings ensures understanding and validates their experience. It shows engagement and encourages further exploration.

  • The value of feedback: Providing constructive feedback, focused on specific behaviors and impact, is essential for growth and progress.

  • The importance of follow-up: Regular follow-up demonstrates commitment and provides an opportunity to track progress and offer further support.

“The Coaching Habit” provides a practical framework for developing a coaching mindset. It emphasizes that coaching isn’t about fixing problems but about empowering individuals to discover their own solutions and take ownership of their lives. By mastering the seven questions and embracing the underlying principles, readers can develop stronger relationships, improve communication, and unlock their own and others’ potential for growth and achievement. The book isn’t about becoming a certified coach; it’s about incorporating a coaching approach into daily interactions to create a more empowering and productive life. The emphasis on asking insightful questions, active listening, and empowering others are core tenets that can be applied to both professional and personal life, leading to more effective communication and lasting positive change.
