Book Summaries

Originals by Adam Grant

How non-conformists move the world
Career And Business
Author

Adam Grant

Adam Grant’s “Originals: How Non-Conformists Move the World” explores the psychology of originality and provides a practical guide to becoming a more creative and impactful individual. The book challenges the common perception that originality requires inherent genius or reckless abandon, instead proposing that it’s a skill nurtured through deliberate practice and a nuanced understanding of risk-taking. Grant argues that originals aren’t necessarily the first to an idea, but rather those who successfully champion and implement novel concepts, often in the face of resistance.

A core theme is the importance of finding the right balance between conformity and dissent. Grant emphasizes that true originality isn’t about blind rebellion, but rather about strategically challenging the status quo. He highlights the power of “calculated risk-taking,” urging readers to analyze potential downsides, develop contingency plans, and choose battles wisely. This isn’t about recklessness; it’s about understanding your own risk tolerance and assessing the potential payoff against the potential cost.

The book introduces the concept of procrastination as a strategic tool, but only when applied judiciously. Grant suggests that some level of procrastination allows for more thoughtful consideration and better integration of diverse perspectives. This isn’t the procrastination of avoidance, but rather the “productive procrastination” that allows for a more refined and resilient idea to emerge after deliberate reflection and information gathering. The key is to avoid procrastination that leads to missed deadlines, but to leverage it as a tool for improved decision-making.

Developing an environment conducive to originality is another crucial aspect. Grant emphasizes the importance of building a supportive network of collaborators who can provide constructive feedback and challenge your assumptions. He highlights the need for psychological safety, where individuals feel comfortable expressing dissenting opinions and taking calculated risks without fear of retribution. This includes both the individual cultivating self-compassion and fostering an environment where others are allowed to express unconventional ideas.

Grant also underscores the importance of embracing failure as a learning opportunity. Originals are not defined by their lack of failures, but rather by their resilience in the face of setbacks. He advocates for developing a “growth mindset,” viewing failures as valuable feedback rather than evidence of incompetence. This involves learning from mistakes, adapting strategies, and persevering even when faced with adversity.

Cultivating self-belief is equally critical. Grant argues that many original ideas are initially met with skepticism or resistance. To overcome this, individuals need to develop a strong sense of self-efficacy and believe in the value of their contributions. This self-belief isn’t arrogance, but rather a realistic assessment of one’s capabilities coupled with a commitment to persevere.

The book provides actionable advice throughout, offering specific strategies:

  • Preparation precedes originality: Originals often appear spontaneous, but their success stems from years of dedication and preparation. They invest significant time in research, learning, and developing relevant skills.
  • Embrace the power of “weak ties”: Building relationships with individuals outside your immediate circle can expose you to new perspectives and ideas, fostering innovation.
  • Find your voice: Originals communicate their ideas clearly and persuasively. This requires honing your communication skills and learning to tailor your message to different audiences.
  • Use the “test-and-learn” approach: Don’t be afraid to experiment and iterate on your ideas. Be willing to adapt and refine your approach based on feedback and results.
  • Don’t wait for permission: While seeking feedback is crucial, don’t let the fear of rejection or criticism paralyze you. Take initiative and act on your ideas.
  • Practice self-compassion: Embrace failure as a learning opportunity, and avoid harsh self-criticism. Self-compassion fuels resilience and encourages persistence.
  • Build a network of supporters: Surround yourself with people who believe in you and provide constructive feedback.

The book is not about being disruptive for the sake of being disruptive. It is about identifying problems that truly need solving and offering innovative solutions. It’s about understanding the context, building strong arguments, and communicating your ideas effectively. Grant emphasizes the importance of aligning originality with purpose, ensuring that innovations contribute to something meaningful and impactful.

In conclusion, “Originals” provides a comprehensive framework for developing originality as a skill. It’s not about inherent talent but a blend of calculated risk-taking, strategic procrastination, resilience, and a conscious effort to cultivate a growth mindset and build a supportive environment. The book empowers readers with practical tools and strategies to become more creative, impactful, and ultimately, more original in their approach to life and work. By embracing these principles, readers can move beyond conformity and contribute meaningfully to the world around them.
